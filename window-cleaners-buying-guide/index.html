How to choose a window cleaners

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best window cleaning kit starts with a good squeegee, choose one with a comfortable brass or plastic channel, replaceable rubber, and a handle that suits the job. Pair it with a microfiber or natural-pulp scrubber and a bucket with a filter if you can, and you will get streak-free results every time without the window cleaner’s markup.

What to look for in a window squeegee: channel, rubber, and handle

The heart of any window cleaning kit is the squeegee itself. Focus on three parts: the channel (the metal or plastic piece that holds the rubber), the rubber (sometimes called the blade), and the handle. A brass channel is heavier and stays straight longer, making it the professional’s choice, it costs more but lasts for years. Plastic channels are lighter and very budget-friendly, fine for occasional use, but they can bend under heavy pressure. The rubber should be soft enough to conform to glass but firm enough to wipe water away in one clean pass. Most good squeegees use a standard-size rubber that you can replace once it gets nicks or wears down. As for the handle, look for something that feels balanced in your hand; Ettore and Unger both offer handles with a slight angle that reduces wrist strain. Don’t overlook the end caps: they keep the channel from scratching window frames and should fit snugly. A squeegee that lets you swap rubber quickly (a channel with a screw or clip fastening) saves headaches when the blade dulls mid-job.

How to choose between a handheld and a pole-mounted system

If you are washing windows that you can reach standing on the ground, a handheld squeegee with a 12- to 14-inch channel is the most efficient choice. That size covers standard residential panes without being too heavy to control. For larger floor-to-ceiling windows, consider a telescoping pole that lets you clip the same squeegee head onto a long handle. Pole systems are sold separately, though some brands like Unger offer complete kits with a pole, squeegee, and scrubber. For second-story windows or skylights, a water-fed pole system (one that delivers filtered water and has a brush at the end) is the safest option, you clean without a ladder. Those kits are significantly more expensive, but for a homeowner who wants one solution for everything, they’re worth the investment. Be honest about how often you will use the tool. If you clean windows twice a year, a simple handheld squeegee and a ten-dollar scrubber are plenty. If you do it quarterly or take on friends’ houses, a pole kit saves your back.

Why you need a scrubber and bucket (and which materials work best)

A squeegee can only pull water from a clean surface, so the scrubber is just as important. The best scrubbers have a thick, absorbent pad on one side and a scrubbing surface on the other. Microfiber scrubbers are excellent because they trap dirt and hold a lot of soapy water; they’re also machine-washable. Natural-pulp scrubbers (the sponge-like ones) are cheaper but wear out faster and can scratch if they pick up grit. For the bucket, a standard mop bucket works, but a purpose-built window cleaning bucket with a filter and a sloping bottom helps control water flow. The filter prevents lint and debris from getting on the glass. Several Unger and Ettore kits include a bucket with a built-in filter – they’re a few dozen dollars more than a plain bucket but genuinely reduce streaks. Use a mild dish soap or a dedicated window cleaning concentrate (never hydrofluoric acid or ammonia mixes meant for industrial use). A few drops of soap in warm water is all you need. Adding a splash of vinegar can help cut hard-water spots, but test it on a small area first.

Squeegee rubber types: which one should you pick?

Rubber for squeegees comes in a couple of common materials: natural rubber, silicone, and a blend called EPDM. Natural rubber gives the cleanest wipe on smooth glass, but it can degrade quickly if left in direct sunlight or stored in a hot car. Silicone lasts longer and resists ozone cracking, but some users find it leaves a slight residue if the glass is very dirty. EPDM is a nice middle ground, it is more durable than natural rubber and still wipes cleanly. Most professionals use natural rubber and replace it every few weeks, but for a homeowner, EPDM or silicone will last through a season or more. The thickness matters too: a standard 3.5mm strip is best for most windows. Thicker rubber (5mm or more) is used on rough surfaces or to push heavier water, but on standard glass it can feel stiff and leave lines. Stick with the standard thickness unless you are cleaning heavily textured glass.

Additional features that actually matter: channel design, end caps, and frame materials

A squeegee’s channel design influences how well it glides. Some channels are open on one end (called “open channel”) so you can slide the rubber out without disassembling the whole thing. Others are closed, which is fine if you replace the entire blade assembly at once. For a beginner, an open channel makes maintenance simpler. End caps protect window frames and prevent scratching; look for soft plastic or rubber caps that fit tightly. They are not all standard sizes, so if you buy a budget brand, check that the caps don’t fall off after a few uses. Frame materials matter for feel and durability. Brass feels solid but adds weight; aluminum is lighter but can dent if dropped; high-impact plastic is fine for occasional use but may crack in cold weather. A good entry-level kit uses an aluminum or brass channel with a comfortable plastic handle. Finally, some kits include a small squeegee (6 to 8 inches) for smaller panes or hard-to-reach spots. That’s a nice bonus, but not essential if you own a normal-size squeegee.

Budget vs. professional: when to spend more?

If you clean windows once a year and just want streak-free glass without fuss, a budget kit (usually under a few hundred dollars for a full setup) will do the job. Brands like Ettore’s Master Squeegee line or Unger’s Nifty system are reliable without being costly. The key is to replace the rubber when it gets nicked, even on a budget tool. A professional-grade kit, from brands like Sorbo or Unger’s Pro series, costs more but includes a brass channel, a comfortable ergonomic handle, and often a guarantee. If you have a lot of large windows or take on commercial work, the extra rigidity and better balance are worth it. A pro kit also holds its value if you decide to sell it later. Do not spend extra on a “starter kit” that includes microfiber cloths you already have or a bucket you already own. Buy the squeegee, a scrubber, and a bucket separately if that’s cheaper. The total price for a good homeowner setup should be well under a few hundred dollars, and it will last for years if you care for it.

Frequently asked questions

What size squeegee should I use for residential windows?

A 12- to 14-inch channel is the sweet spot for most home windows. Small panes (like casement or grid windows) work better with an 8- or 10-inch squeegee. The larger the channel, the more water it moves per pass, but it also becomes harder to control on narrow frames.

How do I prevent streaks when using a squeegee?

Streaks usually come from three things: dirty rubber, too much soap in the water, or pulling the squeegee at the wrong angle. Wipe the rubber between each pass with a clean microfiber cloth, use just a few drops of soap in a bucket, and keep the squeegee at a consistent 45-degree angle while pulling it in a smooth, straight line.

How often should I replace the rubber on my squeegee?

For regular home use, replace the rubber every three to six months, or sooner if you see nicks, wear, or it starts leaving lines. Professionals change it every few weeks because they use it daily. If you notice your squeegee dragging or skipping, that is the first sign the rubber is gone.

Can I use a window squeegee on car windows or shower doors?

Yes, a small squeegee (8 to 10 inches) works well for car windows and shower doors. Be extra careful on car windshields with a defroster grid, the rubber can catch on the wires if they are raised. For shower doors, use a dedicated shower squeegee with a softer blade to avoid scratching glass coatings.

Do I need a special bucket with a filter to get streak-free windows?

Not strictly necessary, but a bucket with a filter does make the job easier. It catches lint and debris so they don’t transfer to the glass. A simple mop bucket plus a microfiber cloth to wipe the rubber between passes works nearly as well for a fraction of the cost.

How should I store my window cleaning squeegee?

Hang it by the handle or lay it flat so the rubber doesn’t get bent. Never store a squeegee on its rubber edge, as that will flatten or pinch the blade, causing streaks. Keep it out of direct sunlight if possible, UV light degrades rubber over time.

In shortA good window cleaning kit doesn’t have to cost much, focus on a well-made squeegee with replaceable rubber, a scrubber that holds water without dripping, and a bucket that keeps your solution clean. Skip the fancy gadgets and spend the saved money on a quality rubber strip that you change regularly. With proper technique and a little practice, you’ll get professional results every time, no ladder required.
