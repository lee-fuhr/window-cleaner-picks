Window Cleaners questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A window cleaning kit typically includes a squeegee, scrubber, and bucket, but the key to streak-free results is the squeegee itself. Look for a brass or stainless steel channel with a soft, natural rubber blade, and pair it with a good microfiber scrubber and the right technique. You don't need to spend a lot, but investing in a quality squeegee from brands like Unger or Ettore makes all the difference.

What should I look for in a window cleaning squeegee?

Start with the channel, the metal piece that holds the rubber. Brass is the gold standard because it resists corrosion and holds the blade firmly, but it costs more. Stainless steel is a close second; avoid cheap plastic channels, which bend or warp over time. The handle should be comfortable and long enough to keep your hand away from the glass. Rubber blade material is just as important. Natural rubber (often labeled “university grade” or “professional”) is softer and conforms better to glass, giving cleaner wipes. Synthetic rubber is harder and lasts longer but tends to skip and streak. Most pros prefer natural rubber and replace it often. Size also matters: a 12- or 14-inch blade is versatile for most residential windows, while 10-inch handles tight casements.

Is it better to buy a window cleaning kit or individual tools?

For most homeowners, a kit is the easiest way to get started. A typical kit includes a squeegee, a scrubber (often called a T-bar or mop), a bucket, and sometimes a small bottle of concentrate. Brands like Unger and Ettore sell reliable “starter” kits that cover the basics for interior windows. The trade-off is that you’re locked into that brand’s parts and sizes. If you plan to clean windows often or have large panes, buying individually lets you choose a higher-quality squeegee and a separate scrubber with a microfiber sleeve. Many pros prefer to pair an Unger squeegee with an Ettore scrubber, because each excels in different areas. You also get to pick a separate bucket with a wheeled caddy, which makes moving around easier for exterior work.

What type of squeegee rubber works best?

Natural rubber is the winner for most glass. It’s softer, has more “snap,” and leaves fewer streaks. The downside: it wears out faster, expect to change it every 10 to 15 full-house cleanings. Synthetic rubber (typically PVC or silicone blends) is harder, lasts longer, but can skip on slightly dirty glass and tends to chatter on dry edges. Ignore the numbers and labels on the blade unless you’re buying in bulk; just look for “natural rubber” on the package. The best-known brand is Ettore’s “Master Squeegee” rubber, which many pros swear by. Also, always buy rubber that’s a few millimeters wider than your channel’s width, it’s easier to trim than to stretch.

How do I use a squeegee without leaving streaks?

The secret is technique, not gear. First, wet the glass thoroughly with your cleaning solution and scrub with a microfiber mop or sponge. Then, before you squeegee, wipe the blade dry on a clean towel. Start at the top corner, pull the squeegee straight across in a smooth, continuous stroke, then slide down and overlap the previous stroke by about an inch. Don’t lift the blade mid-stroke. Always angle the squeegee slightly, about 15 to 20 degrees, so the water runs to the side and doesn’t drip back. For very large windows, many pros use a “fanning” technique: start at the top, curve the squeegee in a long arch, then finish with a straight horizontal pull at the bottom. And never squeegee a dry window; always start with a wet surface.

What else do I need besides a squeegee?

A good scrubber is essential. Choose one with a thick, microfiber sleeve that holds plenty of water and agitation. A standard 14-inch T-bar scrubber works for most windows. You’ll also need a bucket large enough to submerge the scrubber, plus a few microfiber cloths for detailing edges and sills. Some people use a gentle dish soap like Dawn, but a dedicated window-cleaning concentrate (often a small amount of ammonia or surfactant) rinses cleaner. For second-story windows, an extension pole made of lightweight aluminum or carbon fiber makes a huge difference. Get one that telescopes from about 4 to 12 feet. And always keep a bottle of clean water and a few rags handy for quick touch-ups on the corners.

How often should I replace the squeegee rubber?

Replace the rubber as soon as you see streaks, skipping, or a permanent curve at the tip. With natural rubber, that’s about every three to four months for weekly use, or after roughly 15 to 20 full-house cleanings. Synthetic rubber can last twice as long but will eventually harden and skip. You don’t need to replace the whole squeegee, just the rubber strip. Most brands sell replacement rubber in bulk spools or precut lengths. It’s also a good idea to clean the channel and blade after each use: rinse with water and pat dry to prevent buildup that can damage the rubber’s edge.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use dish soap in my window cleaning solution?

Yes, a few drops of a degreasing dish soap like Dawn in a bucket of water works fine for most windows. The downside is it can leave a faint film on very hot days or with hard water. A dedicated window-cleaning concentrate rinses cleaner and won’t streak, but soap is a perfectly good budget option.

How do I clean and store my squeegee after use?

Rinse the channel and blade under warm water to remove soap residue, then wipe dry with a microfiber cloth. Never store a wet squeegee in a closed bucket or drawer, this softens the rubber and can create a permanent curl. Hang it vertically (many kits include a hanger loop) or lay it flat on a towel.

What size squeegee should I get for my windows?

For most residential windows, a 12- or 14-inch blade is ideal. A 10-inch squeegee is better for small casement windows or panes with mullions. Larger panes, like sliding glass doors or picture windows, can benefit from an 18- to 24-inch squeegee, but those are harder to control without practice.

Can I use a squeegee on tinted or coated windows?

Yes, but use a very soft natural rubber blade and avoid excessive pressure. Harsh hard-water deposits or gritty dirt can scratch the film or coating. Always rinse thoroughly before squeegeeing, and consider using a microfiber cloth for the final wipe instead if the coating is delicate.

Do I need distilled water for streak-free windows?

Not strictly, but it helps enormously if your tap water is hard. Minerals in hard water leave spots as they dry. If you’re getting streaks despite good technique, try using a distilled water rinse for the final squeegee pass. You can also install a small portable deionizer system, but that’s overkill for most homes.

How do I prevent drips on the sill or floor when squeegeeing?

After each horizontal stroke, wipe the blade’s end with a dry microfiber cloth before starting the next stroke. Also, tilt the squeegee slightly so water runs to the leading edge and off the glass instead of dripping down. For floor protection, lay down an old towel or a small mat under the window.

In shortA quality window cleaning kit doesn’t have to be expensive, but investing in a good squeegee with natural rubber and combining it with proper technique will save you time and frustration. Whether you buy a complete kit or pick individual tools, focus on the squeegee’s build and the scrubber’s absorbency. With practice, you’ll get crystal-clear results every time, without the need for professional help.
